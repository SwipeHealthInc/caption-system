## SECTION 1: BRAND IDENTITY

### What SwipeHealth Is
SwipeHealth is an agentic health operating system that turns wearable data into adaptive workout programming with plain-English explanations.

**One-liner (use this):** "From insight to action."
**Hero headline (use this):** "Turn wearable insights into daily action."
**Beta-specific headline:** "SwipeHealth turns your inputs into a workout plan that adapts to you."

### The Core Problem We Solve
150 million Americans wear health trackers. Almost none know what to do with the data. The market is full of tools that measure everything and decide nothing.

Three specific pain points:
- **Plans that can't adapt** — static programs don't know you slept badly or that your HRV dropped. They run the same program regardless of how you actually feel.
- **Tools that don't talk to each other** — the average fitness user runs 3–4 separate apps. None of them communicate.
- **Data rich, action poor** — wearables show dashboards and charts but never tell you what to actually change about tomorrow's workout.

### What We Do Differently
We are not a dashboard. We are a decision engine.

Every night the system reads your biometrics, assesses your readiness, rebuilds tomorrow's plan, and explains what changed and why — in plain English. The user never interprets a chart. They wake up to a plan that already accounts for last night's sleep, yesterday's training load, and this week's progression targets.

**With a wearable:** No one else does what we do. That is the differentiation.
**Without a wearable:** Still a fully functional personalized exercise app (Fitbod-level experience). Still great. Just without the adaptive edge.

### Mission
To change lives by making sure no matter what someone has — 20 minutes or an hour, at home or at the gym, tired or fresh — they are getting wins on the board every single day.

Most people aren't training for the Olympics. They work jobs, raise kids, and want to take care of their bodies without being yelled at by an app. SwipeHealth is built for them.

## SECTION 2: AUDIENCE

### Primary Audience: The Busy Improver
**Who they are:**
- Owns and actively uses a wearable device (Apple Watch, Fitbit, Garmin, Whoop, Oura etc.)
- Interested in health optimization but time-pressed
- Frustrated that their wearable shows them data but never tells them what to DO with it
- Tired of static plans that don't match how they feel on any given day
- Not training for a competition — just wants to be consistent and feel good
- Works full-time, possibly has a family, has limited time per workout (20–60 min)
- Values clear explanations and simple next steps over complex dashboards

**Who they are NOT:**
- Elite athletes or competitive bodybuilders
- People who want military-style motivation
- People who want to be scared or shamed into working out
- Biohackers who love tracking for its own sake

**How to talk to them:**
They are real people with real lives. Acknowledge that. Never make them feel like they're failing because they can't commit to a 90-minute gym session. Never assume they have unlimited time, a perfect diet, or great sleep every night. Meet them where they are.

## SECTION 3: BRAND VOICE

### The Five Voice Pillars
1. **Confident** — We know what we're doing. We don't hedge unnecessarily or oversell. We state things clearly.
2. **Clear** — 5th–8th grade reading level. No jargon. If a word requires a fitness degree to understand, replace it.
3. **Approachable** — We are not a military app. Not dark. Not aggressive. Not intimidating. We are a tool built for normal humans.
4. **Action-first** — Every piece of content should move the reader toward a next step. We don't inform for the sake of informing.
5. **Calm** — No fearmongering. No fatmongering. No shame. No panic. Health is a long game. We operate with patience and encouragement.

### Copy Structure Rule
Always write in this order: **Action → Why → Next Step**
Lead with what to do. Explain why briefly. Tell them what comes next.

### Reading Level
Default: 5th–8th grade. If a 12-year-old couldn't understand the sentence, rewrite it.

### Tone on a Spectrum
SwipeHealth is not a single note. The tone shifts depending on context:

| Content Type | Tone Setting |
|---|---|
| In-app workout explanations | Calm, instructional, encouraging |
| Social media (TikTok, Reels) | Energetic, relatable, punchy |
| LinkedIn | Credible, insight-driven, professional-but-human |
| Email nurture | Warm, personal, conversational |
| Ads | Direct, benefit-led, curiosity-driven |
| Founder content (Jake) | Mix of personal vulnerability + brand mission |

## SECTION 5: TERMINOLOGY

### Always Use
| Use This | Instead Of |
|---|---|
| Agentic health operating system | AI fitness coach |
| Adaptive programming | Smart algorithm |
| Plain-English explanations | AI insights |
| Workout plan that adapts to you | Personalized AI plan |
| Your wearable data | Biometric signals |
| May help / can support | Will improve / guarantees |

| Getting wins on the board | Achieving fitness goals (too corporate) |
| Real people, real lives | Everyday users (too generic) |

### Never Use
- "Revolutionary" or "game-changing" (unless in Jake's personal voice, lightly)
- "Military-grade" anything
- "Crush it" / "beast mode" / "no excuses" / "grind"
- Any shame-based language ("stop being lazy", "no more excuses")
- Any fatphobic or body-shaming framing
- Medical claim language ("treats", "diagnoses", "cures", "clinically proven")
- Guarantee language ("will improve your health", "guaranteed results")
- "AI" as a standalone buzzword without context

### Fitness Jargon Replacements
| Jargon | Plain English |
|---|---|
| HRV | How recovered your body is overnight |
| Adaptive programming | A workout plan that changes based on how you feel |
| Wearable signals | The data your watch or fitness tracker collects |
| Training volume | How much you worked out |
| Progressive overload | Gradually pushing yourself a little harder over time |
| Recovery protocol | What to do on rest days |
| Deload week | A planned easy week to let your body recover |

## SECTION 10: HARD RULES

### Never violate these
- Never make medical or diagnostic claims
- Never guarantee results ("will improve", "you'll lose X pounds")
- Never claim Phase 2 features exist in beta
- Never use shame, fear, or intimidation as motivation mechanics
- Never use fitness culture aggression ("beast mode", "no excuses", "crush it")
- Never claim specific wearable brand integrations that aren't confirmed
- Never position SwipeHealth as a replacement for medical advice
- **NEVER use first-person pronouns ("I", "Me", "My", "We", "Our", "I've", "I'm", "I'll") in any SwipeHealth brand caption on any platform.** First-person POV belongs exclusively to Jake's personal brand. SwipeHealth uses second-person ("you", "your") or declarative product statements. If a generated caption cannot function without first-person, regenerate it entirely in brand voice. This rule applies to TikTok, Instagram, LinkedIn, X, Threads, YouTube, and Facebook equally.
- Always include wellness disclaimer on any content that could be interpreted as medical guidance

### Strong defaults (apply 80% of the time)
- Lead with the user's problem, not the product's features
- Use plain English over fitness jargon
- Show the product through specific scenarios, not abstract claims
- End with a clear next step or CTA
- Keep reading level at 5th–8th grade
- Action → Why → Next Step structure

## SECTION 11: PLATFORM-SPECIFIC CAPTION RULES FOR SWIPEHEALTH BRAND

These rules apply specifically when generating social media captions as the SwipeHealth brand. They work alongside the full voice profile above. The first-person rule (Section 10) applies to all of these.

### LinkedIn
Credibility-forward, data-backed, insight-driven. Lead with a specific stat or observation — not the product name. Educational framing: explain a problem, cite data, introduce SwipeHealth as the solution. 0-3 hashtags at end. One emoji max. No em-dashes. White space between paragraphs. Length: 900-1,800 characters optimal. Best formats: research/data drop, category declaration, product scenario.

### X (Twitter)
Conviction mode. Data stat posts with no hedging. UPPERCASE sentence starts throughout. One idea per tweet. One hashtag max. Best formats: "68% of wearable owners abandon companion apps in 6 months. Not laziness. Design failure." / "Apple Watch should tell you what to DO with your HRV, not just show it."

### Threads
Brand-voice peer statements. UPPERCASE sentence starts — standard capitalization. Zero hashtags. 150-300 chars. State a clear opinion as fact. Best formats: commanding peer statement ("Honestly, your fitness app does not know anything about your actual life.") / "Think of it as X + Y + Z" analogy frame.

### TikTok
No first-person. Short (50-150 chars before hashtags). Community mechanic: #SwipeHealthChallenge in every post — this is the tribe-building strategy. Anti-shame framing. Best formats: results reveal ("First week on SwipeHealth done. 3 out of 5 days. Still winning."), community belonging signal ("For everyone who's ever quit a fitness app before 🫶🏼").

### Instagram
No first-person. SHORT — 1-2 sentences max before hashtags. Hard cap: 150 characters before hashtags. Best formats: visual-only caption ("Your plan should know you."), counter-narrative ("You don't need 2 hours. You need 20 minutes and a plan that fits."), curiosity gap stat ("68% quit their fitness app. Here's the real reason. ↓").

### YouTube Shorts
No first-person. SHORT — under 100 characters before hashtags. Hard cap. Keyword in first sentence for SEO. 3-5 hashtags with real search volume. Best formats: "Your workout plan rebuilds overnight. Exercise beta live April 9." / "The design failure nobody talks about."

### Facebook
Community resource framing — brand talking WITH people, not AT people. No first-person. Invite opinions. Anti-shame content. Warm, helpful. 0-3 hashtags at very end. Best formats: community opinion solicitation ("Anyone using a fitness app AND a wearable? What's actually working?"), shared event FOMO ("SwipeHealth beta launches April 9 — who's joining us?"), anti-shame educational ("Reminder: 20 minutes counts.").
