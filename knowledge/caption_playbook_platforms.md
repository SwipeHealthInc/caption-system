# TIKTOK CAPTION INTELLIGENCE

## What This Corpus Reveals

TikTok captions are NOT the content. The video is the content. The caption exists to:
1. Add one layer the video doesn't show (emotional context, challenge name, product tag)
2. Signal community membership (#12330, #ouraring)
3. Create an open loop that keeps viewers watching

The highest-performing TikTok posts (2.8M likes, 264K likes) share one trait: the caption adds EMOTIONAL CONTEXT the video can't show on its own.

## The 4 TikTok Hook Archetypes That Dominate

### ARCHETYPE T1: The Results Reveal
"The results are in!!!" — Lauren Giraldo, 2.8M likes
- Specific number first ("lost 21 pounds")
- Vulnerability immediately after ("I ate normally at family events — it impacted my results but it's cool")
- Community identity signal (#12330 #fittok)
- Sustainability framing over perfection ("lifestyle change, not a fad diet")
- WHY IT WORKS: Combines aspiration with permission. The viewer gets proof PLUS is told they don't have to be perfect.

### ARCHETYPE T2: The Shock Hook
"My Oura ring knew I had cancer before I did" — 234K likes, 2,769 comments
- One sentence. Full stop.
- Zero explanation in the caption — explanation lives in the video
- Emotion so strong it requires no setup
- WHY IT WORKS: The hook IS the entire caption. Comment section does the rest.

### ARCHETYPE T3: The Relatable Confession
"I caved" — 264K likes, 862 comments
- Self-deprecating opener + emoji signals impulse purchase guilt
- Zero product explanation needed — community already knows
- Lifestyle hashtags (#momsoftiktok #dayinmylife) expand reach beyond niche
- WHY IT WORKS: "I caved" is a universal feeling. Product is secondary to the emotion.

### ARCHETYPE T4: The Community Belonging Signal
"For all my 12-3-30 babes out there!" — 116K likes, 600 comments
- In-group language creates instant identity match
- Emoji warmth (#🫶🏼) signals no judgment
- Hashtag IS the community name
- WHY IT WORKS: Readers self-select. If you're in the group, this is for you. If not, you want to join.

## TikTok Caption Rules (Distilled)

**Length:** 50–150 characters before hashtags. If you need more than 150 chars, the video isn't doing its job.

**Hashtag strategy:** 
- Mix: 1 challenge/community tag (#12330, #ouraring) + 2–3 niche fitness tags + #fyp
- Community hashtags are the most powerful TikTok-specific mechanic — they build tribes
- #fyp still works as a reach signal but don't rely on it alone

**Emotional tone:** The caption should feel like something you'd text a friend. Not a brand announcement. Not a product description.

**The open loop:** End with "..." or an emoji that implies "watch to see what happened." Never fully explain in the caption what the video shows.

**SwipeHealth TikTok opportunity:**
- Create a named challenge format (#SwipeHealthChallenge or equivalent)
- "First week on SwipeHealth" confession format — results reveal structure
- Empathy confession hooks: "I finally stopped feeling guilty about short workouts"

**Jake TikTok opportunity:**
- Founder confession format: "I haven't paid myself in 7 months and here's what that actually feels like"
- Results reveal: "30 days building a health app with no salary — here's what happened"
- Community signal: "For everyone who's quit a fitness app before" 

## TikTok Hard Rules
- NEVER write a full product description in the caption
- NEVER use corporate language — read it as if texting a friend
- NEVER use em-dashes
- The caption should be able to stand alone as a single text message
- Hashtags go at the end, separated from the caption text

# INSTAGRAM CAPTION INTELLIGENCE

## What This Corpus Reveals

Instagram at scale is almost entirely VISUAL-FIRST. The top 3 posts (215M views, 96M views, 32M views) have captions that are literally described as "visual-first post — caption is the video itself." The caption text is near-zero.

This tells us something critical: **for Instagram Reels, the hook is in the first frame, not the first line of text.**

However — the Ladder posts (12.5M views, 5.6M views) show that when a talking-head or narrative format is used, captions DO matter and follow specific rules.

## The 3 Instagram Caption Archetypes

### ARCHETYPE I1: Visual-Only (Reels/Carousels)
Posts: Fittonomy (215M, 96M views), Lyfta (32M views)
- Caption is 0–10 words or a visual description
- The ENTIRE hook lives in the first frame
- "Wrong vs. right" split-screen = instant comprehension without reading
- Loop mechanics (seamless loops, muscle highlight overlays) drive watch time
- **For SwipeHealth/Jake:** Product demo or "before/after plan" format using this visual-first approach

### ARCHETYPE I2: Counter-Narrative Hook (Talking Head)
Posts: Ladder ft. Dr. Robin Barrett (12.5M views, 5.6M views)
Caption structure:
```
[BOLD COUNTER-CLAIM that challenges dominant belief]

[Simple reframe — what's actually needed]

[Curiosity CTA that drives profile visits]
```
Example: "You DON'T need to train 7 days a week & eat salads to get toned & strong. Just dumbbells & 30 min. Let me show you how."
- WHY IT WORKS: Emotional relief for overwhelmed viewers + "let me show you" CTA pulls to profile

### ARCHETYPE I3: The Curiosity Gap + Mystery Method
Post: Ladder (5.6M views)
- Specific result first ("down 3 inches", "waist snatched")
- Mystery method named but not explained ("4-2-1 combo moves")
- CTA that invites participation ("try them with me!")
- WHY IT WORKS: Deliberate ambiguity drives comments asking for clarification — algorithm signal

## Instagram Caption Rules (Distilled)

**For Reels (primary format for SwipeHealth/Jake):**
- Caption is secondary. First 3 seconds of video = your hook
- Keep caption under 125 chars (before "more" cutoff on mobile)
- Use the caption to add emotional context the video doesn't show
- One CTA: save, follow, try this

**For Carousels (secondary format):**
- First slide IS the hook — treat it like a LinkedIn first line
- Caption can be longer (up to 300 chars) and adds context
- "Save this" CTA is the highest-performing carousel mechanic
- Swipe prompt in caption: "Swipe to see the full breakdown →"

**Hashtag strategy:**
- 5–10 hashtags after a line break
- Mix niche (#WearableFitness #AdaptiveWorkout) + mid-size (#FitnessApp #WorkoutTips) + brand (#SwipeHealth)
- No hashtag stuffing — relevance over quantity

**SwipeHealth Instagram opportunity:**
- "Wrong vs right" format for fitness plans: "Same workout every day ❌ vs plan that adapts to your life ✅"
- Counter-narrative: "You DON'T need 2 hours at the gym. You need 20 minutes and a plan that fits."
- Mystery method: "The reason 68% of people quit fitness apps (it's not what you think)"

**Jake Instagram opportunity:**
- Confession + results format: "I stopped tracking macros and started tracking wins. Here's what changed."
- Counter-narrative founder take: "Nobody building a fitness app talks about this"
- Behind-the-scenes: First frame = something visually interesting from the build process

## Instagram Hard Rules
- First frame of video = do not waste it on a logo or intro
- Never start caption with brand name or "I"
- Save CTA for carousels, Follow CTA for Reels
- Never explain in the caption what the video already shows
- No em-dashes

# X (TWITTER) CAPTION INTELLIGENCE

## What This Corpus Reveals

X is the most credibility-stratified platform in this corpus. The posts that performed best (15K–25K likes from Karpathy, 8K–15K likes from Bryan Johnson) work because of WHO is posting, not just WHAT they're posting.

However — the structural patterns are completely transferable even without massive authority. The key is: **X rewards conviction + specificity above all else.**

## The 4 X Caption Archetypes

### ARCHETYPE X1: The Definitive Verdict
Post: Andrej Karpathy — "Finding the Best Sleep Tracker" (15K–25K likes)
Structure:
```
[What I tested — specific and extreme]
[TLDR verdict upfront — ranked, decisive, no hedging]
[Link for depth]
```
"I wore 4 sleep trackers every night for 2 months. TLDR Whoop >= Oura > 8Sleep >> Apple Watch"
- WHY IT WORKS: Extreme commitment signals authority. TLDR upfront respects the reader's time. Ranked verdict invites debate.

### ARCHETYPE X2: The Data Flex + Actionable Framework
Post: Bryan Johnson (10K–20K likes, 8K–15K likes)
Structure:
```
[Extraordinary claim with specific number]
[What's notable — 2–3 bullet specifics]
[The "power laws" — numbered, actionable, concrete]
[Optional: visual proof]
```
"I completed 8 consecutive months of 100% sleep. Here's what I think had the greatest effect: 1) RHR below 45 bpm = guaranteed perfect sleep..."
- WHY IT WORKS: Extreme proof + exact numbers + numbered framework = bookmark fuel
- The specificity of "45 bpm = perfect sleep, 50 bpm = 20% worse" is what makes it shareable

### ARCHETYPE X3: The Verdict-First Thread
Post: Dr. Sian Allen (3K–8K likes)
Structure:
```
[I did X experiment for Y months — here's everything I found] 🧵
TLDR:
- [Verdict 1 — bold, no hedging]
- [Verdict 2 — bold, no hedging]  
- [Verdict 3 — bold, no hedging]
[Thread continues with evidence]
```
- WHY IT WORKS: TLDR upfront creates emotional reactions before the evidence. People who disagree reply before reading the thread.

### ARCHETYPE X4: The Product Hot Take
Post: Sid (@immasiddx) — Apple should make a WHOOP-style tracker
Structure:
```
[Bold declarative claim — what should happen]
> [Reason 1 — crisp, one line]
> [Reason 2]
> [Reason 3]
> [Reason 4]
[Tribal closer — the implication]
```
"Apple should release a WHOOP-style fitness tracker. > same sensors > no subscription > no screen > cheaper. This would kill every other fitness tracker."
- WHY IT WORKS: The "should" framing invites both agreement AND opposition. Bullet format is native to X.

## X Caption Rules (Distilled)

**Single tweet (≤280 chars):**
- Lead with the most specific, verifiable, or controversial claim you have
- One idea. One sentence if possible.
- No hedging. No "I think maybe." State it.
- No hashtags unless joining a trending conversation

**Thread format:**
- Tweet 1 = TLDR verdict first, not last
- Use the "I did X for Y [time period]" credibility anchor in tweet 1
- Numbered frameworks in tweet 2+ (Bryan Johnson's "5 power laws" format)
- End thread with an invitation to disagree: "What am I missing?"

**The > bullet format (X-native):**
- Use > (not bullets or dashes) for listed points
- Each point = one line, no explanation
- Explanation comes AFTER the list in a separate tweet

**SwipeHealth X opportunity:**
- Verdict post: "I've tested 6 fitness apps for the last 90 days. TLDR: [ranking]. Here's why."
- Hot take: "Apple Watch should tell you what to DO with your HRV, not just show it."
- Data post: "68% of wearable owners abandon their companion app in 6 months. Not laziness. Design failure."

**Jake X opportunity:**
- Conviction post: "Static workout plans are a design failure. Not a motivation problem."
- The "I did this for X months" credibility anchor: "7 months without a paycheck building a health app. Here's what the data says."
- Hot take: "Most fitness apps are built for someone who doesn't exist."

## X Hard Rules
- NEVER start with "I", "So", "Well", "Just"
- NEVER hedge with "I think maybe" or "it seems like"
- NEVER use em-dashes
- NEVER use more than 1 hashtag in a single tweet
- If it doesn't work as a standalone sentence, it's not a good X post

# YOUTUBE SHORTS CAPTION INTELLIGENCE

## What This Corpus Reveals

YouTube Shorts is the most SEO-driven short-form platform in this corpus. The top performers (The Quantified Scientist, Shervin Shares, Mrwhosetheboss) all use a specific formula:

**Authority signal + Extreme commitment + Definitive verdict = YouTube Shorts hit**

The caption is secondary to the title on YouTube — but both work together for search discovery.

## The 3 YouTube Shorts Caption Archetypes

### ARCHETYPE Y1: The Scientific Comparison
Posts: The Quantified Scientist (widely cited, high engagement)
Structure:
```
[What I tested vs. what — specific devices]
[Method signal — reference equipment used]
[What the test revealed — specific accuracy numbers]
```
- WHY IT WORKS: "Lab-grade credibility" frame positions amateur review as scientific — builds authority no other creator has
- Specific accuracy percentages (73%, 86.5%) are the most shareable element

### ARCHETYPE Y2: The Honest Review + Value Question
Post: Julia Caban — "Is It WORTH $500? After 30 Days"
Structure:
```
[Product name + THE VERDICT QUESTION: Is it worth $X?]
[Honest + specific time frame = triple trust signal]
[What's covered: features, comparisons, definitive verdict]
```
- WHY IT WORKS: "Is it worth $X?" is the most common pre-purchase search query. This video IS the answer.
- "Honest" + specific time frame signals unbiased review

### ARCHETYPE Y3: The Extreme Commitment + Winner
Post: Shervin Shares — "I Wore 18 Fitness Trackers"
Structure:
```
[Absurd scale hook — I tested X (extreme number)]
[Method — what was tested against what]
[Verdict — definitive winner]
```
"I Wore 18 Fitness Trackers... This One's Best!"
- WHY IT WORKS: Extreme commitment = instant authority. Pattern interrupts the standard "top 3" format.

### ARCHETYPE Y4: The Forbidden Knowledge Hook
Post: Mrwhosetheboss pattern
Structure:
```
[Brand] doesn't want you to know this.
OR
You've NEVER seen [product type] like this.
```
- WHY IT WORKS: Conspiracy-curiosity psychology — implies insider information
- Works best with 2–3 broad hashtags only

## YouTube Shorts Caption Rules (Distilled)

**The title is more important than the caption on YouTube.**
Unlike other platforms, the title (not the caption) is what drives search discovery. Caption reinforces and adds secondary keywords.

**Keywords in first line — non-negotiable:**
- Include the primary search term in the first 10 words
- "Personalized workout app for wearable users" not "Finally, something that works"
- Think: what would someone type into YouTube Search to find this video?

**Caption structure:**
```
[Primary keyword + core claim — 1 sentence]
[Supporting context — 1–2 sentences max]
[Subscribe CTA or link to full video]
[3–5 search-optimized hashtags]
```

**Subscribe CTA is platform-native:**
Unlike every other platform, "Subscribe for more" is expected and accepted on YouTube. Use it.

**Hashtag strategy:**
- 3–5 hashtags with real search volume
- #WorkoutApp #FitnessTracker #WearableFitness #AdaptiveWorkout #SwipeHealth
- Avoid generic (#fitness #health) — too competitive, no targeting value

**SwipeHealth YouTube opportunity:**
- "Is SwipeHealth Worth $22.99/Month? After 30 Days" format (when beta users exist)
- "Why Your Fitness App Doesn't Know You Slept Badly" — SEO-rich problem statement
- "Personalized Workout App Review: What Makes It Different" — search-intent aligned

**Jake YouTube opportunity:**
- "Why I Quit a $40M Business to Build a Health App" — career pivot + authority signal
- "I Tested 6 Fitness Apps. Here's What's Actually Missing." — Karpathy-style verdict
- "Building a Health Startup With No Salary: Month 7 Update" — build-in-public SEO

## YouTube Shorts Hard Rules
- Include at least one searchable keyword in the first sentence
- "Subscribe for more" or "Watch the full breakdown" CTA expected
- 3–5 hashtags minimum — this is the one platform where hashtags drive discoverability
- Never use vague openers ("Finally!" "Check this out!") — SEO-hostile
- No em-dashes

# THREADS CAPTION INTELLIGENCE

## What This Corpus Reveals

Threads is the most personality-driven platform in this corpus. The posts that work best are either:
1. Commanding peer-to-peer statements (Gymshark: "seriously, get back in the gym")
2. Personal experiment verdicts with clear opinions (Diego Jimenez: "Apple Watch + Athlytic wins for me")
3. Pattern-interrupt viral moments from other platforms (Daily Mail airplane runner)

The key insight: **Threads rewards opinions stated as facts, not questions.**

## The 4 Threads Caption Archetypes

### ARCHETYPE TH1: The Commanding Peer Statement
Post: Gymshark — "seriously, get back in the gym" (thousands of likes)
- No capitalization, no punctuation — deliberately casual
- Imperative mood from a "tough love friend" position
- Zero context needed
- WHY IT WORKS: Feels like a text from someone who actually knows you. Not a brand talking. A friend.

### ARCHETYPE TH2: The AI Feature Announcement (Warm)
Post: Google — Fitbit AI health coach announcement
Structure:
```
[What's coming + when]
[Triple identity framework: "Think of it as X + Y + Z"]
[Link]
```
- WHY IT WORKS: "Think of it as..." is a powerful analogy bridge for complex tech
- Positions one feature as three benefits

### ARCHETYPE TH3: The Pattern-Interrupt Viral Moment
Post: Daily Mail — airplane bathroom 5K runner
- Story told in one sentence + absurd detail
- Reaction language embedded ("sparking equal parts amazement and debate")
- WHY IT WORKS: Pre-loaded controversy — reader already knows the comment section will be entertaining

### ARCHETYPE TH4: The Personal Experiment Verdict
Post: Diego Jimenez — Whoop vs Apple Watch experiment
- "I tested X vs Y" personal frame
- Clear methodology (VO2 Max tests, Dexa scans) adds credibility
- Definitive verdict at end — no hedging
- WHY IT WORKS: The verdict IS the shareable insight. "Apple Watch + Athlytic wins for me" is what gets quoted.

## Threads Caption Rules (Distilled)

**Tone:** Peer-to-peer, always. Not brand-to-audience. Friend-to-friend.

**Length:** 150–300 characters is optimal. One clear thought, stated clearly.

**No hashtags.** Threads does not reward hashtag use. Zero. None.

**Capitalization:** Lowercase-casual works better than proper capitalization here. Gymshark's "seriously, get back in the gym" performs better than "Seriously, Get Back In The Gym."

**The Threads energy:**
- Thinking out loud
- Personal verdict ("Apple Watch wins for me")
- Behind-the-scenes
- Low-stakes observation that feels true

**What doesn't work on Threads:**
- Formal brand announcements
- Long explanations
- Multiple points in one post
- Anything that feels like it was written by a marketing team

**SwipeHealth Threads opportunity:**
- "honestly, your fitness app doesn't know anything about your actual life" (Gymshark energy)
- "Think of SwipeHealth as a fitness trainer, a schedule-adapter, and a plain-English explainer — all in one"
- "most fitness apps were built for someone who doesn't exist" (simple verdict)

**Jake Threads opportunity:**
- Build-in-public updates in lowercase casual tone
- "7 months in. no salary. still the best decision I've made." 
- Personal experiment verdicts: "tested 4 fitness apps last month. here's what's actually different."
- Thinking-out-loud observations: "the fitness industry has a six-pack problem and nobody's fixing it"

## Threads Hard Rules
- Zero hashtags
- Never use formal brand language
- Never open with a question — state the opinion, let the replies question it
- 500 character limit per post — most posts should use less than half of that
- No em-dashes

# FACEBOOK CAPTION INTELLIGENCE

## What This Corpus Reveals

Facebook's corpus is the most instructive for what NOT to do. Two of the five posts are cautionary tales (Peloton apology, community outrage post). This tells us Facebook communities have the most activated tribal loyalty — one wrong move and the community turns on you.

The posts that work: **Community invitation > Brand announcement. Every time.**

## The 4 Facebook Caption Archetypes

### ARCHETYPE F1: The Shared Event FOMO
Post: Peloton — Post Malone Artist Series (19K views, 524 likes, 231 comments)
Structure:
```
[Anticipation language — "breaks the mold"]
[Celebrity/cultural crossover — pulls multiple fan communities]
[Multi-person tagging — activates each person's audience]
[Specific date/time — creates urgency without fake scarcity]
```
- WHY IT WORKS: Shared event + specific timing + celebrity crossover = community moment people don't want to miss

### ARCHETYPE F2: The Teaser/Scarcity Post
Post: Garmin India — teaser that was pulled (cross-platform virality)
- Vague teaser + mysterious image + quick removal = Streisand Effect
- "Coming sooner than you think" + no specifics = compulsive speculation in comments
- WHY IT WORKS: The removal amplified the virality. The ambiguity forced people to theorize.

### ARCHETYPE F3: The Community Opinion Solicitation
Post: Anonymous Garmin Facebook Group — "Anyone wear both a Garmin and a Whoop?" (100–300+ comments)
Structure:
```
[Simple comparison question]
[Personal context — "I've heard good things about both"]
[Zero opinion stated — invites everyone to be the expert]
```
- WHY IT WORKS: No wrong answer. Every person has a tribe. Low friction to comment.
- This format works in groups MORE than on brand pages

### ARCHETYPE F4: The Cautionary Tale (What NOT to Do)
Posts: Peloton body-shaming post + community outrage post
- Setting unrealistic standards = immediate community backlash on Facebook
- The Facebook community is the most activated when its values are violated
- Peloton's inclusivity promise is deeply held — breaking it activated hundreds of critical comments
- **For SwipeHealth:** Never post anything that implies a "correct" body type, pace, or fitness level. Facebook is where this will hurt most.

## Facebook Caption Rules (Distilled)

**Audience:** 35–55 skew. More life experience. More skepticism. More brand loyalty when earned.

**Tone:** Warm, community-first, helpful resource. Not a company talking AT people. A brand talking WITH people.

**Length:** 150–300 words for value posts. Shorter (50–100 words) for event/announcement posts.

**Hashtags:** 0–3 maximum. Facebook is not a hashtag platform. If used, keep them at the very end.

**The most important Facebook rule:**
Don't violate the community's values. On Facebook, the community WILL mobilize. SwipeHealth's values (no shame, real people, real lives, no six-pack required) need to be consistent with every post. One perceived inconsistency and the comment section turns.

**What drives Facebook engagement:**
- Opinion solicitation ("Anyone else use both X and Y? Which wins for you?")
- Shared event with specific date/time
- Community pride ("We're proud of our community for X")
- Educational content framed as helpful, not promotional

**What kills Facebook engagement:**
- Pure promotional posts with no community angle
- Body standards or pace standards content (see: Peloton example)
- Corporate language that feels like a press release
- Posts that ignore the audience's life context (time, family, work)

**SwipeHealth Facebook opportunity:**
- Community opinion: "Anyone using a fitness app AND a wearable? What's actually working for you?"
- Shared event: "SwipeHealth beta launches April 9 — who's joining us?"
- Helpful resource: "The real reason people quit fitness apps (and what actually works instead)"
- Anti-shame: "Reminder: 20 minutes counts. A short workout is not a failed workout."

**Jake Facebook opportunity:**
- Less relevant for Jake's personal brand — Facebook skews older than Jake's primary audience
- If used: founder story format, community question, or SwipeHealth mission post
- Never use Jake's casual "brother" energy here — this audience expects more polish

## Facebook Hard Rules
- Never imply a "correct" body type, fitness level, or pace
- Never use shame or guilt mechanics — this community will mobilize against it
- Always frame SwipeHealth as a community resource, not a product
- No em-dashes
- Never pure promotional content — always lead with community value

# CROSS-PLATFORM SYNTHESIS

## The 5 Patterns That Work Everywhere

1. **Specificity beats vagueness on every platform.** "Lost 21 pounds" beats "great results." "45 bpm resting heart rate" beats "good sleep stats." "8 consecutive months" beats "a long time." Whatever platform, whatever format — specific numbers win.

2. **The strongest hooks are either counterintuitive OR the answer to a question people are actively asking.** TikTok's "My Oura ring knew I had cancer before I did" is counterintuitive. YouTube's "Is It WORTH $500?" is the answer to an active search query. Both work. Generic hooks ("Check out my latest post!") work nowhere.

3. **Community identity beats product features everywhere.** "#12330 babes" on TikTok. "Gymshark" on Threads. "Garmin vs. Whoop" tribes on Facebook. "Oura ring community" on Instagram. People want to belong to something, not buy something.

4. **Definitive verdicts outperform hedged opinions.** "Apple Watch + Athlytic wins for me" (Threads) gets more engagement than "I think Apple Watch might be slightly better in some cases." Bryan Johnson's "45 bpm = perfect sleep, 50 bpm = 20% worse" outperforms "lower resting heart rate tends to correlate with better sleep." State the verdict. Let the comments debate.

5. **The platform's native casualness level is the ceiling.** You can't be more casual than the platform allows, but you also can't be more formal. Threads is lowercase casual. LinkedIn is professional-human. TikTok is a text to a friend. Facebook is a community conversation. Violate the native register and the post feels wrong before anyone reads the content.

## Platform-Specific Opportunity Map for Jake + SwipeHealth

| Platform | Jake's Biggest Opportunity | SwipeHealth's Biggest Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| TikTok | Founder confession + results reveal | Named community challenge (#SwipeHealthChallenge) |
| Instagram | Counter-narrative talking head Reel | "Wrong vs right" visual for static vs adaptive plans |
| X | Conviction posts + definitive verdicts | Data stat posts (68% abandon rate, $22.99 vs $200/session) |
| YouTube Shorts | Career pivot story + build-in-public SEO | "Is SwipeHealth Worth It?" honest review format |
| Threads | Build-in-public lowercase casual updates | Simple opinion verdicts about fitness app failures |
| Facebook | Not primary for Jake — skip or repurpose LinkedIn | Community opinion solicitation + anti-shame content |

## The Single Most Important Finding Across All Platforms

**The "real person, real life" angle is completely unoccupied in the fitness/wearable niche across every platform.**

TikTok's top posts are transformation reveals (for people who finished the challenge) or product flexes (Oura cancer detection, Bryan Johnson's perfect metrics). Instagram's are visual-first form guides. X's are credentialed experts with extreme commitment. YouTube's are comprehensive lab tests. Threads' are brand statements or tech verdicts.

Nobody is consistently speaking to the person who:
- Has 20 minutes not 60
- Works a full-time job and has kids
- Doesn't want to be perfect — just consistent
- Doesn't own a $300+ wearable
- Feels guilty about skipping workouts

SwipeHealth owns this positioning. Jake's personal story embodies it. The content gap is real and it spans every platform.
