# CAPTION SYSTEM RULES — APPLY TO ALL PLATFORMS

These 4 guardrails override everything else. Apply on every platform, for both brands, on every caption.

**RULE 1: First-Person POV**
SwipeHealth brand captions: NEVER use "I", "Me", "My" as first-person subjects. SwipeHealth speaks as a product/brand — second-person ("you", "your") or declarative statements. If a caption cannot work without first-person, regenerate it entirely.
Jake personal brand captions: First-person IS correct and expected. His story, his voice.
Test: if the account posting is @SwipeHealth → no first-person. If it's Jake personally → first-person is right.

**RULE 2: X / Threads Sentence Casing**
All X and Threads captions: sentences start UPPERCASE — standard capitalization throughout. Applies to both Jake and SwipeHealth on both platforms. Tone is achieved through word choice, NOT lowercase casing.
Correct: "Honestly, your fitness app does not know anything about your actual life."
Wrong: "honestly, your fitness app doesn't know anything about your actual life"

**RULE 3: YouTube Caption Length Split**
SwipeHealth brand: SHORT — under 100 characters before hashtags. 1-2 sentences max.
Jake personal: LONGER — 2-4 sentences fine. Include "Subscribe for more" CTA (platform-native).
Never apply the long format to SwipeHealth. Never apply the short format to Jake when the story needs more.

**RULE 4: Instagram Caption Length — Both Brands**
Both Jake and SwipeHealth on Instagram: SHORT — 1-2 sentences max before hashtags. Hard cap: 150 characters before hashtags. Brevity > Length. Always.

# SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

## What Makes These Captions Work

Three things separate the top performers from the rest across all 46 posts:

**1. The hook earns the read in one sentence.**
Every caption above ~200 reactions opens with a line that either (a) names a problem the reader already feels, (b) makes a bold claim they want to verify, or (c) drops a specific number that signals credibility. No warm-up. No preamble. Full power in sentence one.

**2. Specificity is the engine of believability.**
"Reducing cycle-related score drops by 81 percent." "9 years ago I started building." "99.1 percentile." "Two hours of AI saved me £2,000." Vague claims get skipped. Specific numbers get shared, questioned, and discussed.

**3. Comment volume comes from identity, debate, or "add your own."**
The highest-comment posts (Justin Welsh: 1,075; Dan Lee: 882; Tejas Gawande: 947) are engineered to trigger self-identification, disagreement, or participation. The highest-reaction posts (Satya Nadella: 8,730) rely on authority and brevity. For Jake and SwipeHealth — mid-tier creators without Satya's platform — comment engineering matters more than reaction farming.

## Strongest Recurring Themes
- Data gap: people have wearable data but no action path
- Consistency over perfection: small daily wins beat heroic effort
- Myth-busting: challenging oversimplified health metrics with nuance
- Build-in-public: founder vulnerability + progress drives community
- "The era of X is over": bold category-shift declarations

## What Matters Most to Replicate This
1. Open with a specific claim or number — not a brand name or "I"
2. White space is mandatory — dense paragraphs kill LinkedIn posts
3. Engineer for comments by choosing a topic with a clear "what do YOU think?" angle
4. Match credibility signals to the audience (researchers cite studies, founders cite outcomes)
5. One CTA only — ask one thing, get a response

# SECTION 2: CAPTION ARCHETYPES

The 46 posts collapse into 8 repeatable archetypes. Every caption Jake or SwipeHealth produces should be assigned to one of these before writing begins.

## ARCHETYPE 1: The Metric Explainer
**Definition:** Takes a confusing health/tech metric and explains it in plain English. Builds trust by teaching.
**Best examples:** Will Ahmed (HRV), Andy Galpin (HRV), Euan Black (HRV measurement nuance)
**Comment style:** Questions, "I've been wondering this," appreciation
**Hook pattern:** Question or "Most people don't understand X"
**SwipeHealth use:** Explaining HRV, recovery scores, adaptive programming to general audiences
**Jake use:** Positioning himself as an expert via education

## ARCHETYPE 2: The Bold Thesis
**Definition:** Stakes a strong, verifiable claim about a category shift or market truth. Invites debate.
**Best examples:** Heiki Riesenkampf ("Fitness App Era Is Over"), Andrew Ng (AI bubble), Mike G. Hansen (is personal training dying?)
**Comment style:** Agreement, disagreement, "here's my take"
**Hook pattern:** Declarative statement + dramatic implication
**SwipeHealth use:** "The static workout plan era is over." "Your wearable data has been useless. Until now."
**Jake use:** Contrarian founder takes on fitness culture, app design, content creation

## ARCHETYPE 3: Build-in-Public Update
**Definition:** Shares honest progress on something being built. Invites the audience to follow the journey.
**Best examples:** Amy Zhou (AI jewelry), Marco Altini (9-year milestone), Navdeep Singh (NeetBot launch)
**Comment style:** Encouragement, questions about the build, "I've been following this"
**Hook pattern:** Announcement + honest acknowledgment of difficulty
**SwipeHealth use:** Launch milestones, waitlist updates, technical decisions made in public
**Jake use:** Founder journey, sacrifices being made, what he's learning

## ARCHETYPE 4: The Personal Metric Moment
**Definition:** Turns a single data point (a recovery score, a streak, a HRV reading) into an identity or behavior story.
**Best examples:** Varun Duggirala (yellow recovery score), Bryan Johnson (99.1 percentile), Dr. Megan Tranter (Oura score 96 after rest)
**Comment style:** "Mine too," "What's your protocol?", sharing their own scores
**Hook pattern:** Specific metric + emotional reaction
**SwipeHealth use:** User scenario content showing how the app adapts to real signals
**Jake use:** Personal transparency about his own health data and what it means

## ARCHETYPE 5: The Research/Data Drop
**Definition:** Shares a study, survey, or data set with clear practical implications. Authority by citation.
**Best examples:** ŌURA (pregnancy study), Alistair Brownlee (wearable research), Nick Bloom (AI survey)
**Comment style:** Shares, saves, follow-up questions
**Hook pattern:** Study + specific stat + "here's what this means"
**SwipeHealth use:** Market data about wearables (68% abandon rate), fitness app market size, health behavior research
**Jake use:** Citing research to support SwipeHealth's positioning claims

## ARCHETYPE 6: The Myth-Buster
**Definition:** Challenges a common assumption using domain expertise or research. Generates respectful disagreement.
**Best examples:** Stacy T. Sims PhD (HRV for women is more complex), Cailbhe Doherty (recovery scores are black boxes)
**Comment style:** Debate, nuance additions, "I didn't know this"
**Hook pattern:** "The common advice is X. But actually..."
**SwipeHealth use:** "Your fitness app isn't failing you. It just wasn't built for your life."
**Jake use:** Challenging fitness culture norms (six-packs, beast mode, static plans)

## ARCHETYPE 7: The Practical List
**Definition:** Offers a numbered or bulleted list of actionable items. Comment volume comes from "add your own."
**Best examples:** Chris Walker (5 ways to improve HRV), Satya Nadella (5 prompts), Heather Murray (AI workflow)
**Comment style:** "I'd add X," "Number 3 is underrated," sharing their own list
**Hook pattern:** "Here are [N] ways to [desired outcome]"
**SwipeHealth use:** "3 things your recovery score is actually telling you." "5 signs your workout plan isn't built for your life."
**Jake use:** Tactical lists from his experience in marketing, fitness, building SwipeHealth

## ARCHETYPE 8: The Quantified Case Study
**Definition:** Uses real personal data over a significant time period to tell a transformation story.
**Best examples:** Caroline Cahill Canty (5 years of Oura data), Heather Murray (2 hours, £2,000 saved)
**Comment style:** "What's your setup?" "How do you track this?" Inspiration
**Hook pattern:** Time duration + specific metric + counterintuitive outcome
**SwipeHealth use:** Beta user results, founder's own data, specific before/after scenarios
**Jake use:** Career metrics (60+ awards, billions of views) reframed as consistency lessons

# SECTION 3: HOOK LIBRARY

## The 10 Highest-Performing Hook Formulas

### HOOK 1: The Specific Number Drop
Open with a precise, surprising number. No context first — the number IS the hook.
```
Template: [Specific number] [surprising claim or outcome].
Examples:
- "9 years ago I started building HRV4Training."
- "My one-month avg daily Recovery Score: 99.1 percentile."
- "Five years of Oura data. 12,500 hours of sleep tracked."
- "Today I used AI for 2 hours. It saved me £2,000."
Jake version: "7 months without a salary. Here's what I know now."
SwipeHealth version: "68% of wearable owners abandon companion apps in 6 months."
```

### HOOK 2: The Bold Declaration
State a category-shift claim as fact. No hedging.
```
Template: [The X Era Is Over / X is dead / X doesn't work anymore].
Examples:
- "The Fitness App Era Is Over."
- "Personal training is losing its appeal."
- "Stop comparing yourself to others."
Jake version: "Static workout plans are broken. Here's why."
SwipeHealth version: "Your fitness app isn't failing you. It just wasn't designed for your actual life."
```

### HOOK 3: The Question That Challenges an Assumption
Ask the question your audience already has but hasn't voiced.
```
Template: [Common experience]? But [challenge to assumption].
Examples:
- "If you use a wearable… you check your readiness score. But how much can you trust that number?"
- "Is personal training a dying industry?"
Jake version: "You've been tracking your sleep for months. But does your workout actually know that?"
SwipeHealth version: "Your watch told you how you slept. What did your workout plan do with that?"
```

### HOOK 4: The Counterintuitive Reframe
Take something the audience thinks they understand and flip it.
```
Template: [Common belief]. [Counterintuitive truth].
Examples:
- "This is a complicated metric for women." (challenging HRV = recovery assumption)
- "Rest isn't lazy." (with 96 Oura readiness as proof)
- "Most people's 2026 will look exactly like 2025. Not because they can't change — because they won't."
Jake version: "Consistency is the most underrated fitness skill. And the one nobody sells you."
SwipeHealth version: "More data isn't the problem. No one telling you what to do with it is."
```

### HOOK 5: The Exciting Announcement (with honest caveat)
Lead with the announcement, immediately acknowledge the hard truth.
```
Template: [Exciting thing]! [But here's the honest reality].
Example: "We're building an AI device that looks like real jewelry. Hardware is hard. Everyone says impossible."
Jake version: "SwipeHealth exercise beta launches April 9. It's not everything we want it to be yet. Here's what it is."
SwipeHealth version: "Waitlist is open. Here's exactly what beta does — and doesn't — do."
```

### HOOK 6: The Empathy Open
Open by naming a specific frustration or moment the audience knows exactly.
```
Template: [Specific relatable moment they've lived].
Examples:
- "For the first time in 3 weeks, my WHOOP recovery score went yellow. Dammit."
- "Models suddenly getting good enough to write most of my code creates complicated feelings."
Jake version: "You set the alarm for 6am. You got up at 7:30. Now your workout plan is the same as yesterday's."
SwipeHealth version: "You slept 4 hours. Your fitness app still wants you to do leg day."
```

### HOOK 7: The Research Anchor
Open with a study or data point and let the implication pull the reader forward.
```
Template: [Research finding with specific number] + [What this means].
Examples:
- "New research introduces HRV-CV. Think: consistent good grades vs one good test."
- "International survey of 5,000+ CFOs, CEOs on AI suggests big expected impacts."
Jake version: "150 million Americans wear a health tracker. Almost none know what to do with the data."
SwipeHealth version: "68% of wearable owners abandon their companion app in 6 months. Not because they stopped caring."
```

### HOOK 8: The Teach-First Open
Define a term or concept as the opening move.
```
Template: What IS [concept]? / [Concept] explained simply.
Examples:
- "What IS heart rate variability? HRV? I get asked this all the time."
Jake version: "What is adaptive programming? Simple version."
SwipeHealth version: "HRV. Recovery Score. Readiness. What do these actually mean for your workout tomorrow?"
```

### HOOK 9: The List Promise
Signal value immediately by promising a specific, numbered list.
```
Template: Here are [N] [things] that [outcome].
Examples:
- "Here are the top 5 ways I've found to improve HRV."
- "Here are 5 prompts that show what's now possible."
Jake version: "3 things I wish someone told me about building a health tech startup."
SwipeHealth version: "5 signs your workout plan isn't built for your real life."
```

### HOOK 10: The Status Flex (Authority-earned only)
Works only when the creator has significant authority. Pure specificity, zero explanation.
```
Template: [Extreme specific claim]. [One follow-up sentence max].
Examples:
- "My one-month avg daily Recovery Score is 99.1 percentile."
Note: Only deploy once Jake has built a significant audience base. Premature use reads as cringe.
Jake version (future): "60+ industry awards. 2 billion views. Now starting over — for a reason."
```

Best for: SwipeHealth brand content, product positioning, awareness posts
```
[Bold hook — 1 sentence]

[CTA — one question or one action]
```

Best for: Bold takes, Jake personal brand LinkedIn, industry commentary
```
[Declarative thesis]

[Evidence 1 — specific example or stat]

[Evidence 2 — different angle on same thesis]

[Evidence 3 — personal or product observation]

[Implication — so what does this mean?]

[CTA — question that invites debate]
```

### STRUCTURE 3: Moment → Feeling → Lesson → CTA
Best for: Personal brand content, build-in-public, founder vulnerability posts
```
[Specific moment — something that happened]

[Honest reaction — how it felt, not how you wish it felt]

[Lesson — what this moment taught you. Keep it humble.]

[CTA — "Has anyone else experienced this?"]
```

Best for: Educational content, HRV/recovery explainers, research drops
```

[Plain-English explanation]

[CTA]
```

```
[Specific detail 1]

[What comes next — honest framing]

[CTA]
```

# SECTION 5: FORMATTING RULES

## Non-Negotiable LinkedIn Rules

1. **White space between every paragraph.** 2-3 sentences max, then blank line. Non-negotiable.
2. **First 210 characters = the only lines that decide reach.** Write them last.
3. **Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.** Use sparingly for the key insight or number.
4. **3-5 hashtags at the very end, never mid-caption.** Niche over broad.
5. **0-2 emojis max.** Purposeful only. Never decorative strings.
6. **Lists work when they invite "what would you add?" participation.**
7. **Never end with "let me know your thoughts."** Use a specific question.

## Platform Formatting Summary

| Platform | Length | Hashtags | Emojis | White Space |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn | 900-1,500 chars | 3-5 at end | 0-2 | Mandatory |
| TikTok | 100-150 chars | 3-5 at end | 1-3 | N/A |
| Instagram | 150-300 chars | 5-10 at end | 2-5 | Recommended |
| X | ≤280 chars | 0-1 | 0-2 | N/A |
| Threads | 150-300 chars | 0 | 1-3 | N/A |
| YouTube Shorts | 150-300 chars | 3-5 at end | 1-3 | N/A |
| Facebook | 150-300 words | 0-3 | 1-3 | Recommended |

# SECTION 6: CTA RULES

## What Drives Comments vs Shares vs Saves

| Goal | Best CTA | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Comments | Specific opinion question | "What metric do you actually pay attention to?" |
| Comments | Debate invitation | "Agree or disagree?" |
| Comments | "Add your own" | "What would you add to this list?" |
| Shares | Content that makes sharer look smart | No explicit CTA needed |
| Saves | Practical list or how-to | "Save this for when you need it" |
| Follows | Build-in-public update | "Follow to watch this build" |

## The 5 CTAs That Performed Best in This Corpus

1. The debate question: "Agree or disagree — is personal training dying?" (57 comments)
2. The experience check: "What does your wearable actually tell you to do?"
3. The comment-gate: "Comment 'WORD' and I'll send you X" (882-947 comments)
4. The community check-in: "What's keeping you going right now?"
5. The add-your-own: "What would you add to this?" (94 comments)

## CTAs That Killed Engagement
- "Like and share if you agree"
- "Let me know your thoughts below"
- Multiple CTAs in one post
- Generic "Drop a comment!"

# SECTION 7: PSYCHOLOGICAL TRIGGERS

## The 7 Triggers That Drive Engagement in This Niche

1. **Identity Threat/Affirmation** — Make the reader feel seen or challenged as a specific type of person
2. **Curiosity Gap** — Imply information without giving it all upfront
3. **Specificity as Credibility** — Exact numbers trigger trust; vague claims trigger skepticism
4. **Shared Frustration** — Name the frustration before offering the solution
5. **Social Proof by Specificity** — Specific outcomes attributed to specific behaviors
6. **Expert Framing Without Gatekeeping** — Share expertise in a way that makes the reader smarter, not smaller
7. **The "Add Your Own" Engine** — Structure content so readers naturally want to add their perspective

# SECTION 8: JAKE'S CONTENT OPPORTUNITY (Gaps in This Niche)

After analyzing all 46 posts, these angles are completely absent — open territory for Jake:

1. **Humor.** Zero posts in the wearables niche used humor. Not even dry wit. Jake's California-warm humor is a genuine differentiator.

2. **The non-athlete POV.** Everyone writes for optimizers and biohackers. Nobody speaks to the person who just wants to work their job, raise their kids, and not feel like they're failing at health. This is SwipeHealth's core audience — uncontested.

3. **Anti-shame fitness content.** The entire wearables niche implicitly assumes the reader wants to maximize performance. Nobody is saying "it's okay to have a yellow recovery day." This is a real gap.

4. **The cost comparison done right.** "Personal trainer at $200/session vs SwipeHealth at $22.99/month" — nobody in this corpus made this argument clearly and compellingly.

5. **The "data is useless without action" macro claim.** Several posts touched the data gap, but no one has owned the thesis: "Wearables built a $40B industry on telling people things they can't act on." Jake can own this.

# SECTION 10: FAILURE PATTERNS TO AVOID

## The 5 Biggest Mistakes in This Niche

1. **Opening with the brand name.** "SwipeHealth announced today..." = instant skip. Lead with the user's problem.

2. **Claiming without proving.** "Revolutionary adaptive technology" triggers skepticism. "Your plan adjusts based on last night's HRV, not last week's template" creates belief.

3. **Writing for the optimizer, not the real person.** The entire wearables niche writes for performance maximizers. SwipeHealth's audience is the dad who works 50 hours a week. Write for them.

4. **Long-form with no white space.** Ideas buried in unbroken paragraphs die. White space is not optional.

5. **Earning the flex before earning the audience.** Bryan Johnson's 99.1 percentile post works because he has 300k+ followers. Jake needs to earn that right with education and build-in-public content first.
