# PLATFORM RULES: All 7 Channels
## Caption Generation System — SwipeHealth & Jake Personal Brand
## Version: 1.1 — March 2026

These rules are injected per platform when generating captions. Each platform section is self-contained. Apply ALL rules in the relevant section for every caption generated on that platform.

# CAPTION SYSTEM GUARDRAILS — APPLY BEFORE ANY PLATFORM RULES

These 4 rules are non-negotiable. They override platform-specific instructions wherever there is a conflict.

**GUARDRAIL 1: First-Person POV — SwipeHealth vs Jake**
SwipeHealth brand: NEVER use "I", "Me", "My" as first-person subjects in any caption on any platform. SwipeHealth speaks as a product/brand using second-person ("you", "your") or declarative statements. Regenerate any caption that cannot function without first-person.
Jake personal brand: First-person IS correct and expected. His story, his voice.
If the account is @SwipeHealth → no first-person. If it's Jake personally → first-person is right.

**GUARDRAIL 2: X / Threads Sentence Casing**
All X and Threads captions for both brands: sentences start UPPERCASE — standard capitalization throughout. The peer-to-peer casual tone is achieved through word choice and directness, NOT lowercase casing.
Correct: "Honestly, your fitness app does not know anything about your actual life."
Wrong: "honestly, your fitness app doesn't know anything about your actual life"

**GUARDRAIL 3: YouTube Caption Length Split**
SwipeHealth brand YouTube Shorts: under 100 characters before hashtags. Hard cap. 1-2 sentences max. No first-person.
Jake personal YouTube: 2-4 sentences appropriate for build-in-public and career pivot content. Include "Subscribe for more" or "Watch the full breakdown" CTA — it is platform-native on YouTube.
Do not apply the long format to SwipeHealth brand. Do not apply the short format to Jake personal when the story warrants more.

**GUARDRAIL 4: Instagram Caption Length — Both Brands**
Instagram captions for both Jake and SwipeHealth: 1-2 sentences max before hashtags. Hard cap: 150 characters before hashtags. Brevity > Length. Always. If 3+ sentences are generated, cut to 1-2. If 2 sentences, does the second add a new layer? Cut if redundant.

# PLATFORM 1: TIKTOK

## Core Behavior
TikTok is a discovery platform. Most viewers do NOT follow you yet. The caption exists to reinforce the hook in the video and drive follows, saves, and comments. Captions are secondary to the video — they support, not lead.

## Character Limit
- Hard limit: 2,200 characters
- Optimal: 100–150 characters
- Rule: Keep it short. TikTok users do not read long captions. Get in, get out.

## Hook Rules
- The hook is in the VIDEO, not the caption
- Caption hook (first line) should either: (a) mirror the video hook to reinforce it, or (b) add a layer of intrigue that makes the viewer want to watch again
- Do NOT restate what the video already shows — add context or curiosity

## Structure
```
[One punchy line that adds to or reinforces the video]
[Optional: 1 short sentence of context or CTA]
[3–5 hashtags]
```

## Tone
- Casual, conversational, scroll-native
- Short sentences. Fragments are fine.
- Match the energy of the video — don't be formal if the video is casual
- Never sound corporate or rehearsed

## Hashtag Strategy
- 3–5 hashtags maximum
- Mix: 1 broad (#fitness or #health), 1 niche (#wearabletech or #workoutapp), 1 trend if relevant
- Do NOT stuff hashtags — TikTok's algorithm doesn't reward hashtag stuffing the way Instagram used to
- Place hashtags at the end, never mid-caption

## CTA Style
- Soft CTAs work best: "Save this", "Drop a comment", "Follow for more"
- Hard sells ("Click link in bio to buy") perform poorly organically — use sparingly and only when running paid
- Question CTAs drive comments effectively: "What does your watch say about your sleep?"

## Emoji Usage
- 1–3 emojis max
- Use only if they add meaning or energy — not as decoration
- Never use emojis that feel corporate or forced

## What Works on TikTok
- Relatable real-life scenarios ("You slept 4 hours and the app still wants you to do leg day")
- Contrarian takes ("Your fitness app is lying to you")
- Before/after or problem/solution framing
- Specific numbers and data points ("68% of wearable users abandon their companion app")
- "POV:" format for personal brand content

## What Doesn't Work
- Long paragraphs
- Formal sign-offs
- Multiple CTAs
- Overly polished corporate language
- Hashtag blocks of 20+

## Jake vs SwipeHealth
- Jake: More personal, more casual, more "founder telling his story"
- SwipeHealth: More product-focused, scenario-driven, benefit-led

# PLATFORM 2: LINKEDIN

## Core Behavior
LinkedIn is a professional network where content is consumed intentionally. Users slow down here. Long captions work — IF they earn attention line by line. The feed shows only the first 2–3 lines before "see more" — those lines are everything.

## Character Limit
- Hard limit: 3,000 characters
- Optimal for engagement: 900–1,800 characters (long enough to deliver value, short enough to finish)
- First 2–3 lines (before "see more"): These are your hook — treat them like a headline

## Hook Rules
- The hook must work standalone — someone should be compelled to click "see more" from the first 2 lines alone
- Best hook types for LinkedIn:
  - Counterintuitive statement ("The fitness app with the most users has the worst retention")
  - Specific number + problem ("150 million Americans wear health trackers. Almost none know what to do with the data.")
  - Personal confession ("I spent 7 months building a company without paying myself. Here's what I learned.")
  - Direct challenge ("Your workout app isn't broken. Your expectations are.")
- No filler warm-up lines. First sentence = full power.

## Structure
```
[Hook — 1–2 punchy lines, no fluff]

[Body — story, insight, or argument. Short paragraphs (2–3 sentences max). White space between every paragraph.]

[Lesson or payoff — what the reader should take away]

[CTA — one question or one clear action]

[3–5 hashtags on final line]
```

## Tone
- Professional but human — not corporate, not bro
- Jake: Can be more personal, vulnerable, founder-journey focused
- SwipeHealth: More insight-driven, credibility-forward, data-backed
- Always sounds like a smart person talking, not a press release
- Contrarian takes work well but must be backed up — no empty hot takes

## Line Break Rules (CRITICAL for LinkedIn)
- Every 2–3 sentences: new paragraph with a blank line
- This is not optional — dense blocks of text get skipped entirely
- Single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis are effective and native to LinkedIn
- Never write 5+ sentences in a row without a break

## Hashtag Strategy
- 3–5 hashtags at the very end
- Use niche-relevant tags (#Fitnesstech, #HealthTech, #WearableTech, #Founder, #SwipeHealth)
- Do NOT use generic tags (#motivation, #fitness, #life) — too broad, no targeting value
- Never mid-caption hashtags

## CTA Style
- End with a question that invites a specific opinion: "What does your wearable actually tell you to do?"
- Or a soft share CTA: "If you know someone stuck in this cycle, send them this."
- Never: "Click here to buy" — wrong platform energy for organic posts
- Polls work well on LinkedIn — consider when relevant

## Emoji Usage
- 0–2 emojis
- Optional — LinkedIn leans professional
- If used: only at start of a bullet or as a subtle accent, never as decoration
- Never a string of emojis

## What Works on LinkedIn
- Data-backed observations about the fitness/health/tech industry
- Founder journey content (struggles, lessons, decisions)
- Contrarian takes on fitness culture with substantiation
- "I noticed X and it changed how I think about Y" structure
- Lists of specific insights (not vague tips)
- Specific real-world scenarios (Jake + HRV story)

## What Doesn't Work
- Motivational fluff ("Believe in yourself!")
- Overly casual language (reads as unprofessional)
- Bragging without context
- Posts that are all product pitch
- Generic fitness advice with no unique angle

## Jake vs SwipeHealth
- Jake: Founder journey, personal lessons, building in public, credibility markers (Forbes 30u30, Inc 5000, UT faculty)
- SwipeHealth: Market insights, product positioning, industry data, customer scenarios

# PLATFORM 3: INSTAGRAM

## Core Behavior
Instagram is a visual-first platform. The caption supports the visual — it does not replace it. Feed posts reward saves and shares most (algorithm signal). Reels work more like TikTok. Stories are conversational. Tailor the caption to the format.

## Character Limit
- Hard limit: 2,200 characters
- Target: 1–2 sentences before hashtags. Hard cap: 150 characters before hashtags (both Jake and SwipeHealth brands)
- Think of it as Threads-length brevity applied to Instagram. The video/visual does the work. The caption adds ONE emotional layer.
- Brevity > Length. Always on Instagram.
- CUT TEST: If 3+ sentences, cut to 1–2. If 2 sentences, does the second add a new layer? Cut if redundant. If 1, usually correct.

## Hook Rules
- Instagram shows first 125 characters before "more" on mobile
- First line must work as a standalone hook
- Best hooks: curiosity gap, relatable moment, bold claim, specific number
- Never start with "I" (algorithm penalizes and it's a weak opener)

## Structure (Feed / Carousel)
```
[Hook — first 1–2 lines, max 125 chars before cut-off]

[Body — short paragraphs, white space between each]

[Payoff or lesson]

[CTA — save, comment, share, or question]

[Line break]
[Hashtags — see below]
```

## Structure (Reels)
```
[1 punchy line that adds to the video]
[Optional: 1 follow-up sentence]
[3–5 hashtags]
```

## Tone
- More visual, more emotional than LinkedIn
- Jake: Can be warm, personal, behind-the-scenes energy
- SwipeHealth: Clean, benefit-driven, aspirational but grounded (not hype)
- Casual enough to feel native, polished enough to reflect the brand

## Hashtag Strategy
- Feed posts: 5–10 hashtags
- Reels: 3–5 hashtags
- Mix: 2–3 niche (#WearableFitness, #AdaptiveWorkout), 2–3 mid-size (#FitnessApp, #HealthTech), 1 brand (#SwipeHealth)
- Place hashtags after a line break at the end — never mid-caption
- Do NOT use 30 hashtags — Instagram's algorithm no longer rewards stuffing

## CTA Style
- "Save this for your next workout"
- "Tag someone who needs this"
- "Drop a 🙋 if this is you"
- Question CTAs to drive comments: "What time of day do you work out?"
- Never aggressive sales language in organic posts

## Emoji Usage
- 2–5 emojis in feed posts
- 1–3 in Reels captions
- Use to break up text, add personality, or emphasize key points
- SwipeHealth: Keep it clean — no excessive emoji chains
- Jake: Slightly more casual emoji use is fine

## What Works on Instagram
- Relatable fitness struggles ("You set your alarm for 6am. You did not get up at 6am.")
- Transformation or before/after narratives (even conceptual, not just physical)
- "Hot take" content that gets saved and reshared
- Aspirational but real — not perfect bodies, real lives
- Behind-the-scenes founder content (Jake building SwipeHealth)

## What Doesn't Work
- Long blocks of text with no breaks
- All-product, zero-story content
- Fitness shaming or perfection culture
- Generic motivational quotes with no original angle
- 30-hashtag blocks

## Jake vs SwipeHealth
- Jake: Personal journey, real moments, founder vulnerability + wins
- SwipeHealth: Product benefits, real-life scenarios, aspirational wellness positioning

# PLATFORM 4: FACEBOOK (SwipeHealth Brand Only)

## Core Behavior
Facebook skews older than TikTok and Instagram (primary: 35–55). Content performs better when it feels genuine and conversational rather than polished and branded. Groups and community sharing drive most organic reach. Meta ads on Facebook are a separate consideration (not covered here — organic only).

## Character Limit
- Hard limit: 63,206 characters (effectively unlimited)
- Optimal organic: 40–80 words for link posts, 150–300 words for value posts
- Rule: Shorter than you think. Facebook users skim. Get to the point fast.

## Hook Rules
- Facebook truncates at ~400 characters before "See More"
- Hook must earn the click
- Works well: relatable scenario, specific stat, direct question to the reader
- Avoid: starting with brand name or product pitch

## Structure
```
[Hook — relatable, direct, specific]
[2–3 short paragraphs of context or value]
[CTA — question, link, or share prompt]
[1–3 hashtags optional]
```

## Tone
- Warmest, most conversational of all platforms
- Feels like a community post, not a brand announcement
- SwipeHealth should sound like a helpful resource, not a company
- No aggressive sales language

## Hashtag Strategy
- 1–3 hashtags maximum — Facebook is not a hashtag platform
- Or none at all — organic Facebook posts rarely benefit from hashtags
- If used: #SwipeHealth #HealthTech #FitnessApp

## CTA Style
- Community-oriented: "Have you tried anything like this?"
- Share-driven: "Share this with someone who needs it"
- Link posts: "Read more at [link]"
- Avoid aggressive CTAs

## Emoji Usage
- 1–3 emojis max
- More conservative than Instagram — this audience skews older
- Use to add warmth, not energy

## What Works on Facebook
- Relatable real-life moments (not hitting the gym after a rough night)
- Community questions and polls
- Educational content framed as helpful tips
- Real scenario storytelling
- Sharing milestones or community proof

## What Doesn't Work
- TikTok-style brevity (too cryptic for this audience)
- Heavy fitness culture language
- Posts that feel like ads

# PLATFORM 5: X (TWITTER)

## Core Behavior
X rewards concision, wit, and strong opinions. It is the most text-native platform. Threads perform well for longer narratives. Single tweets need to land immediately. Controversial or counterintuitive takes get the most amplification.

## Character Limit
- Hard limit: 280 characters (single tweet)
- Threads: 280 per tweet, as many as needed
- Rule: If it can be said in 280 characters, don't make it a thread.

## Hook Rules
- You have 280 characters — the whole thing is the hook
- Every word must earn its place
- Best tweet structures: statement + punchline, observation + implication, question + answer, counterintuitive fact
- Never waste the first word — "I", "So", "Well" are weak openers

## Structure (Single Tweet)
```
[Full thought in ≤280 characters. No hashtags unless trending topic.]
```

## Structure (Thread)
```
Tweet 1: Hook — the most compelling single claim or statement
Tweet 2–N: Evidence, story, or breakdown
Final tweet: Lesson, CTA, or summary
```

## Tone
- Most casual and direct of all platforms
- Jake: Sharp, opinionated, confident — the smartest person at the table who doesn't need to show off
- SwipeHealth: Product observations, data-driven takes, industry commentary
- Wit is welcome — dry humor, understatement, light sarcasm work on X

## Hashtag Strategy
- 0–1 hashtags for organic tweets
- Hashtags on X rarely add value unless joining a trending conversation
- Never use more than 1

## CTA Style
- Implicit CTAs work best: a great tweet gets replies and retweets without asking
- For threads: "Thread 🧵" at the end of tweet 1 signals more is coming
- Explicit CTAs: "Retweet if you agree" — use sparingly, can feel desperate

## Emoji Usage
- 0–2 emojis
- X leans text-native — emojis should add clarity or emphasis, not decoration
- 🧵 for thread signal is standard and expected

## What Works on X
- Counterintuitive observations ("The app with the best UX in fitness has the worst retention")
- Specific stats with a sharp angle
- Hot takes backed by reasoning (not just rage bait)
- Founder building-in-public updates
- Short story + lesson format
- Dry, confident humor

## What Doesn't Work
- Corporate announcements
- Long blocks of text in a single tweet
- Overly emotional content (save that for LinkedIn/Instagram)
- Hashtag stuffing

## Jake vs SwipeHealth
- Jake: More personal opinions, founder observations, wit and humor
- SwipeHealth: Product insights, market observations, data points about the fitness/health space

# PLATFORM 6: THREADS

## Core Behavior
Threads is Instagram's text-based platform. It borrows from X's format but with Instagram's social graph and a warmer, less confrontational culture. It currently rewards authentic, personality-driven content. Less algorithm pressure than other platforms — more community-feel.

## Character Limit
- Hard limit: 500 characters per post
- Threads (multi-post): 500 per post, unlimited posts
- Optimal: 150–300 characters for standalone posts

## Hook Rules
- Threads skews casual and conversational — hooks here are softer than X
- Works well: relatable observation, casual question, behind-the-scenes thought
- Less "hot take" energy than X, more "talking to a friend" energy
- First post in a thread still needs to earn the read

## Structure (Single Post)
```
[One clear, conversational thought under 500 characters]
[Optional: 1 follow-up sentence or question]
[No hashtags needed]
```

## Structure (Thread)
```
Post 1: Conversational hook or observation
Post 2–N: Build on it naturally, as if talking through an idea
Final post: Conclusion, question, or CTA
```

## Casing Rule (Non-Negotiable)
- Standard sentence capitalization required on Threads
- Capitalize the first word of every sentence
- The casual peer-to-peer tone is achieved through WORD CHOICE and directness, not through lowercase styling
- Correct: "Honestly, your fitness app does not know anything about your actual life."
- Wrong: "honestly, your fitness app doesn't know anything about your actual life"

## Tone
- Most conversational of all platforms
- Feels like texting a group of people who are interested in what you do
- Jake: Behind-the-scenes, building in public, casual founder thoughts
- SwipeHealth: Casual product thoughts, relatable fitness observations, light brand presence
- Authenticity over polish here more than any other platform

## Hashtag Strategy
- 0 hashtags — Threads does not use hashtags the same way other platforms do
- Exceptions: if joining a specific Threads community conversation

## CTA Style
- Natural questions: "What do you think?"
- "Agree or disagree?"
- Low-pressure — Threads culture resists hard sells
- Replies and quotes drive reach on Threads more than explicit CTAs

## Emoji Usage
- 1–3 emojis, casual use
- Matches the conversational energy of the platform

## What Works on Threads
- Unfiltered observations about building a startup
- Relatable fitness moments (casual, not motivational poster energy)
- Genuine questions to the community
- Short personal anecdotes
- Repurposed X-style takes, softened in tone

## What Doesn't Work
- Over-polished brand content
- Long-form essays (wrong platform for that)
- Aggressive sales language
- Heavy data posts (save that for LinkedIn)

## Jake vs SwipeHealth
- Jake: Primary voice on Threads — personal, casual, building in public
- SwipeHealth: Lighter brand presence, let Jake's personal voice carry the platform

# PLATFORM 7: YOUTUBE SHORTS

## Core Behavior
YouTube Shorts is a discovery platform like TikTok — but with YouTube's search infrastructure underneath it. Captions here support the video and can include searchable keywords that improve discoverability. Shorts under 60 seconds perform best. The caption has more SEO value here than on any other short-form platform.

## Character Limit
- Hard limit: 5,000 characters
- SwipeHealth brand: SHORT — hard cap of 100 characters before hashtags, 1–2 sentences max
- Jake personal: LONGER — 2–4 sentences, soft cap of 350 characters before hashtags
- Rule: Write for the viewer first (short and punchy), but include searchable keywords naturally

## Hook Rules
- Same as TikTok — hook is in the video, not the caption
- First line of caption should add context or reinforce the video's hook
- Include the main keyword naturally in the first line (for search)

## Structure
```
[Hook line that includes primary keyword naturally]
[1–2 sentences of supporting context]
[CTA — subscribe, comment, or follow]
[3–5 hashtags with search value]
```

## Tone
- Similar to TikTok but slightly more structured
- YouTube audience generally has slightly longer attention spans than TikTok
- Can include slightly more context in the caption

## Hashtag Strategy
- 3–5 hashtags with search value
- Think: what would someone search to find this video? Use those as hashtags
- Example: #WorkoutApp #FitnessTracker #WearableFitness #AdaptiveWorkout
- Place at end of caption

## CTA Style
- "Subscribe for more" works on YouTube in a way it doesn't on other platforms — users are used to this CTA here
- "Drop a comment if this is your situation"
- "Watch the full version [link]" if Short is a clip from a longer video
- Linking to full YouTube video from a Short is an effective strategy

## Emoji Usage
- 1–3 emojis
- More restrained than Instagram, similar to TikTok

## What Works on YouTube Shorts
- Educational snippets ("Did you know your HRV affects your workout?")
- Product demos and explainers
- Clips from longer videos teasing the full version
- Relatable fitness moments with a clear lesson
- Keyword-rich topics that people actually search for

## What Doesn't Work
- Captions with zero keywords (wastes the SEO advantage)
- Captions that just repeat what the video title already says
- Hard sales language in organic shorts

## Jake vs SwipeHealth
- Jake: More personal story, founder journey clips, educational fitness content
- SwipeHealth: Product scenarios, data-backed observations, "how it works" explainers

# CROSS-PLATFORM RULES (Apply Everywhere)

## Universal Hard Rules
- Never fabricate statistics or inflate numbers
- Never make medical claims or imply diagnosis/treatment
- Never use shame or fear as motivation mechanics
- Never claim Phase 2 features exist in Beta (for SwipeHealth content)
- Always match the tone to the platform — never copy-paste the same caption across all channels

## Universal Formatting Rules
- Never use em-dashes (—) — they read as AI-generated
- Minimize bullet points in captions — write in natural language
- Short sentences > long sentences on every platform
- White space is your friend — use line breaks generously
- Never use all caps (except very rare emphasis on X)

## Universal Voice Rules
- Jake's personal content: code-switch based on platform (casual on TikTok/Threads, credibility-forward on LinkedIn)
- SwipeHealth brand content: always confident, clear, approachable, action-first, calm
- Neither voice should sound like a press release
- Neither voice should use AI-giveaway words (delve, unlock potential, game-changer, revolutionary, seamlessly)

## Universal CTA Principles
- One CTA per post maximum
- Platform-appropriate CTA (see individual platform rules)
- Implicit CTAs (great content that naturally makes people respond) beat explicit CTAs (begging for engagement)
- Never use multiple CTAs in one caption
